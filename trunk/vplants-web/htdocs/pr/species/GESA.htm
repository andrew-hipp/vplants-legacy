Plant of Concern
Gentiana saponaria L.
harvestbells, soapwort gentian

Synonyms: Gentiana cherokeensis (W. P. Lemmon) Fern. ; G. puberula Michx. as to type, non Small

Description: Perennial herb, 8 - 70 cm tall. Stems normally unbranched, usually hairless, but sometimes with short, swollen-based hairs. Leaves opposite, stalkless, less than 2 cm wide, dark green. The leaf shape is variable, ranging from linear to elliptic, but usually widest near the middle, possibly blunt-tipped, but never long pointy, always short hairy on the margins. Flowers stalkless, in clusters of 1 - 8 at the top of the stem, but also at the end of branches at the upper part of the stem in larger individuals. Each flower is subtended by a pair of bracts, which are longer than wide. Flowers blue (sometimes violet), open to loosely closed. Calyx tube 5 - 15 mm long and hairless. Sepal tips (calyx lobes) shorter than or about equal to the length of the tube, narrow (0.8 - 4 mm wide), with pointy tips and hairy margins. Petals five, lobes at the tips usually incurved, and seldom exceeding the interconnected membrane (plaits) by more than 2 mm. Plaits about the same length as the petals and evidently jagged or two-toothed at the free ends. Anthers fused together.

Similar Species: A more southeastern species, Gentiana catesbaei, has often been confused with G. saponaria, but it has brighter green, wider leaves, which are widest near the base, not the middle. Also in G. catesbaei, the calyx lobes are longer than the tube and the corolla lobes are usually 2 - 4 mm longer than the alternating plaits. Gentiana saponaria is also often confused with a hybrid, G. x billingtonii, which is a cross between G. puberulenta and G. andrewsii. The hybrid however, tends to have leaves and calyx lobes that are widest near the base where as in G. saponaria, they are widest near the middle. Also, the leaves of the hybrid tend to be stiffer and lighter green in color than the dark green leaves of G. saponaria. Finally, the flowers of the hybrid appear to be a deeper color blue. The species and two subspecies of G. andrewsii may also be confused with G. saponaria, but their leaves are much wider and possess long pointy tips. Also, unlike G. saponaria the calyx lobes of G. andrewsii are much shorter than the connected tube portion.
Gentiana catesbaei, Gentiana x billingtonii, Gentiana andrewsii, Gentiana andrewsii var. andrewsii, Gentiana andrewsii var. dakotica

Flowering: September to mid-November.

Habitat and Ecology: Sandy moist prairies and Black Oak savannas, moist or seasonally wet thickets, open woods, or even along railroad tracks, but overall rare in our area.

Notes: This species is now extirpated (extinct) in Michigan. The species has a relatively wide distribution in the United States and a lot of physical variation occurs between different populations as well as within single populations (Pringle 1967).

Etymology: Gentiana is named after Gentius, king of Illyria, who supposedly discovered a medicinal value for the plant. Saponaria refers to the resemblance of the leaves to the common soapwort, which is in the genus Saponaria.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Extirpated in Michigan
