Invasive Plant
Lythrum salicaria L.
purple loosestrife

Synonyms: Lythrum salicaria L. var. gracilior Turcz.; L. salicaria var. tomentosum (P. Mill.) DC.; L. salicaria var. vulgare DC.

Description: Perennial herb, 0.5 - 1.5 m tall. Stem stout, usually with some hairs, especially near the branching inflorescence. Leaves opposite or whorled, stalkless, more than 4 cm long, widest below the middle and tapering to a pointy tip. Bracts (leaf-like structures) beneath each flower pointy-tipped and hairy. Flowers numerous, in crowded terminal "spikes" or wand-like clusters 10 - 40 cm in length. Individual flowers with 5 - 7 bright purple-red petals and more than ten (usually twelve) stamens.

Similar Species: Another Eurasian species, Lythrum virgatum, which is often cultivated (and escapes) is very similar, but the plant is not hairy. Lythrum salicaria is normally hairy, especially in the flowering "spikes". The other species in our area, L. alatum, is native and usually less than 0.8 m tall. It is not hairy, has a 4-angled stem, alternate leaves less than 5 cm long, and always less than ten stamens per flower.
Lythrum virgatum, Lythrum alatum

Flowering: July - October.

Habitat and Ecology: Common in wet places of all kinds.

Notes: Listed as one of the top 100 of the "World's Worst" invaders, this plant is native to Eurasia and has escaped cultivation to become an aggressive weed in our natural wetlands and even roadside ditches. Large patches of this species are seen throughout the Chicago Region. Though seemingly "pretty" due to their bright purple-red flowers, this plant spreads quickly and chokes out high-quality native wetland plant species, which consequently makes the wetlands less useful for wildlife. These plants persist year after year as well as produce copious amounts of seed. Due to the threat this plant poses to our nation's wetlands, the USDA has spearheaded efforts to combat this species by releasing natural biological enemies, beetles from Eurasia, which feed on Purple Loosestrife.
