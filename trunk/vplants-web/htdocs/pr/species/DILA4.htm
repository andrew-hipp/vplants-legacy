Invasive Plant
Dipsacus laciniatus L.
cut-leaved teasel, cutleaf teasel

Description: Biennial herb with taproot, 0.5 - 3 m tall. Stems stout, erect, branching near the top, yellowish green to green, longitudinally grooved, with hooked and straight spines. The plant persists as a basal rosette the first season, producing a large flowering stalk in the second season. Basal leaves typically fused, irregularly pinnatifid or toothed, and usually dying after flowering begins. Stem leaves opposite, unstalked, to 40 cm long, yellowish green to green, fern-like (pinnatifid), and coarsely hairy. The lower leaves are fused at the base to form a cup, their margins are toothed and prickly, but the upper leaves may be linear and smooth. Inflorescence a densely-flowered terminal head, 3 -10 cm tall, 5 cm in diameter, cylindrical to nearly spherical. Involucral bracts whorled, 2 - 15 cm long, linear, up-curved, often equal to or exceeding the height of the head. Flowers small subtended by a long, stiff, green bract. Calyx 1 mm long, cup-shaped. Corolla 8 - 15 mm long, white, funnel-shaped, with 4 unequal lobes. Achenes 3 - 7 mm long, grayish brown.

Similar Species: The similar Dipsacus sylvestris has purple flowers and linear leaves, while D. laciniatus has white flowers and upper stem leaves with irregularly pinnatifid lobes.
Dipsacus sylvestris

Flowering: Late June to September.

Habitat and Ecology: An aggressive invasive species, Dipsacus laciniatus, is common in disturbed ground and waste areas.

Notes: Dipsacus laciniatus takes over large areas of land, excluding native vegetation in prairies and savannas. Feasible biological control methods do not exist, and mowing and burning have limited success.
Vegetation Management Guideline - Missouri Department of Conservation

Etymology: Dipsacus means having flower heads surrounded by spiny bracts. Laciniatus means divided into narrow lobes.

Regional Status: Non-native. Invasive.
