Family: Gentianaceae | Genus: Gentiana Specimens | Plant Description | Photos
Gentiana andrewsii Griseb.
bottle gentian, closed bottle gentian, closed gentian

Description: Perennial herb, 10 cm - 1.2 m tall. Stem usually unbranched and hairless. Leaves opposite, stalkless, somewhat lance-shaped (3 - 15 cm long, up to 4 cm wide), with somewhat long pointed tips, and rough or prickly-hairy margins. Flowers (1 - 25) stalkless, clustered at the top of the stem and often in the axils of the upper leaves, sky blue, closed and club-shaped. Sepal tips much shorter than the connected tube portion and hairy on the edges. Petals five but joined to longer, toothed or jagged-edged whitish membranes (plaits) between the actual petals. Anthers fused together.

Similar Species: Gentiana andrewsii is represented by two varieties in the Chicago Region. See links below for further information. There are two other species of closed gentians in our area that may be confused with G. andrewsii. Gentiana alba differs by having whitish flowers and completely smooth leaf margins and calyx lobes. Gentiana saponaria has much narrower leaves (< 2 cm wide) with shorter, not long pointed tips, and the free part of the sepals are almost as long as the connected tube portion.

Flowering: August to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Occasional in damp prairies, wet meadows, and other moist open ground.

Notes: The only observed pollinators for this gentian are bumblebees, which appear to be the only insects strong enough and smart enough to open the closed flowers (Costelloe 1988). Specimens mapped and linked at right are those that were not designated a variety name.

Etymology: Gentiana is named after Gentius, king of Illyria, who supposedly discovered a medicinal value for the plant. Andrewsii is named after the English botanical artist Henry C. Andrews.

Regional Status: Native.
