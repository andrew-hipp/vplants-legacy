Gentiana andrewsii var. dakotica A. Nels.
Dakota gentian

Description: Perennial herb, 10 cm - 1.2 m tall. Stem usually unbranched, and occasionally covered with short fuzzy hairs. Leaves opposite, stalkless, somewhat lance-shaped (3 - 15 cm long, up to 4 cm wide), with somewhat long pointed tips, and rough or prickly-hairy margins. Flowers (1 - 25) stalkless, clustered at the top of the stem and often in the axils of the upper leaves, sky blue, closed and club-shaped. Sepal tips much shorter than the connected tube portion and hairy on the edges. Petals five, the free ends triangular to rounded with a pointed tip up to 3 mm long, and the rest of each petal joined to longer, toothed or jagged-edged whitish membranes (plaits) between the actual petals. Anthers fused together.

Similar Species: There are two other species of closed gentians in our area that may be confused with Gentiana andrewsii var. dakotica. Gentiana alba differs by having whitish flowers and completely smooth leaf margins and calyx lobes. Gentiana saponaria has much narrower leaves (< 2 cm wide) with shorter, not long pointed tips, and the free part of the sepals are almost as long as the connected tube portion. In addition to this extremely rare western variety of G. andrewsii, the more common typical variety, G. andrewsii var. andrewsii, occurs in the area. The typical variety has less well-developed petal tips, with the free point scarcely even 1 mm long, and the stems are always completely hairless.
Gentiana alba, Gentiana saponaria, Gentiana andrewsii var. andrewsii, Gentiana andrewsii

Flowering: mid-August to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Low, moist, open ground, but extremely rare in our area.

Notes: In our area, the two varieties of G. andrewsii are very similar. It is west of the Mississippi River that G. andrewsii var. dakotica becomes more easily distinguished (Pringle 1967). It appears the differentiation between the two varieties may be more a matter of geography rather than anything visibly observable from morphology.

Etymology: Gentiana is named after Gentius, king of Illyria, who supposedly discovered a medicinal value for the plant. Andrewsii is named after the English botanical artist Henry C. Andrews. Dakotica refers to the plant occurring in the western United States.

Regional Status: Native.
