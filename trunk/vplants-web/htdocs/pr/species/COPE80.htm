Plant of Concern
Comptonia peregrina (L.) Coult.
sweet fern

Synonyms: Comptonia peregrina (L.) Coult. var. aspleniifolia (L.) Fern. ; Myrica aspleniifolia L. var. tomentosa (Chev.) Gleason ; Myrica aspleniifolia L. ; Myrica peregrina (L.) Kuntze

Description: Shrub, to 1.5 m tall. Twigs yellow-green to reddish brown with resin dots when young, becoming yellow-brown and shiny and finally red-purple to copper. Buds tiny, round, and hairy. Leaves alternate, 3 - 15.5 cm long, 0.3 - 2.9 cm wide, linear, fern-like (pinnatifid), dark green and often shiny above, gray-green and somewhat hairy below, dotted with fragrant glands, persisting into winter. Male and female flowers may be on the same tree (monoecious) or on separate trees (dioecious); male flowers borne in cylindrical catkins, 1.5 - 4 cm long, yellow-green, clustered at the ends of branches; female flowers borne in spherical to elongated catkins, 5 - 7 mm long, with 8 reddish, persistent, linear bracts at base. Fruit is an achene, 3 - 5 mm long, olive-brown, elliptical to conical, surrounded by bur-like bracts, 1 - 2 cm long.

Similar Species: Comptonia peregrina is a spreading shrub with fern-like, aromatic leaves and bur-like achenes that make it distinctive in the Chicago Region.

Flowering: April.

Habitat and Ecology: Forms colonies by spreading rhizomes in sand flats and barrens or other areas with infertile sandy or gravelly soils.

Notes: This is a good plant to use along highways or other sandy, dry, infertile areas. It tolerates infertile soil because nitrogen-fixing bacteria form nodules in the roots, which convert atmospheric nitrogen to a form that the plant can use. Native Americans once used this species as a tonic, stimulant, food seasoning, ceremonial incense, or poison. The leaves can be used as a mosquito repellent when crushed or burned.

Etymology: Comptonia is named after Henry Compton (1632 - 1713), a patron of botany and bishop of London. Peregrina comes from the Latin word meaning exotic or foreign, which refers to the species being foreign to the original European author.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Endangered in Illinois.
