Plant of Concern
Platanthera leucophaea (Nutt.) Lindley

Synonyms: Habenaria leucophaea (Nutt.) A. Gray var. leucophaea; Orchis leucophaea Nutt.; Blephariglotis leucophaea (Nutt.) Farw.

Description: Perennial herb, 30 cm - 1.1 m tall. Leaves stalkless with the base wrapped around stem, lower leaves 10 - 20 cm long, reduced in size going up stem, but always longer than wide. Flowers (5-20) twisted upside down, subtended by a small bract, and arranged on a stalk 8 - 20 cm tall. The flowers are showy, white or creamy-white, and have a thin nectar spur 3 - 5 cm long. Lip of flower projecting forward, deeply 3-lobed and the lobes deeply fringed. Flowers strongly fragrant, especially at dusk.

Similar Species: Platanthera leucophaea is most likely to be confused with P. lacera which has the spur shorter than or equal to the ovary. In P. leucophaea the spur is much longer than the ovary. The closest relative is the western prairie fringed orchid (Platanthera praeclara), which occurs to the west of the Mississippi River and has a different pollinator (Sheviak and Bowles 1986).

Flowering: Late June to early August.

Habitat and Ecology: Almost exclusively in wet prairies though also found in bogs and marshes, but rare overall.

Notes: Platanthera leucophaea was once common throughout Midwestern wet prairies but is now found in fewer than 60 locations. Much of the populations have been lost through destruction or modification of habitat. Conservation research is underway to increase current population sizes and reintroduce this orchid in restored prairies. Hawkmoths are the only known pollinators of P. leucophaea.

Etymology: Platanthera comes from the Latin words platanus, meaning flat or broad, and anthera, referring to anthers, which together means "flat anthers". Leucophaea comes from the Latin words leuco, meaning white, and phaeo, meaning dark or dim, which refers to the off-white flowers.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Endangered in Illinois, Extirpated in Indiana, Endangered in Michigan, Endangered in Wisconsin, Threatened Federally.
