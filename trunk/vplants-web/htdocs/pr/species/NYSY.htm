Plant of Concern
Nyssa sylvatica Marsh.
black gum, blackgum, sour gum, sour-gum, black tupelo, pepperidge

Synonyms: Nyssa sylvatica Marsh. var. caroliniana (Poir.) Fern. ; Nyssa sylvatica Marsh. var. dilatata Fern. ; Nyssa sylvatica Marsh. var. typica Fern.

Description: Tree, 10 - 18 m tall, trunk 20 - 40 cm in diameter, rarely to 40 m tall and 2 m in diameter. Trunk remains undivided far into the conical, cylindrical crown; branches horizontal with lower branches sometimes hanging down and turning upward at the ends. Bark dark gray to nearly black, resembling alligator hide, furrowed with long, deep, irregular ridges that are broken into blocks. Twigs green to light brown and hairy, becoming dark red-brown to gray and smooth with age. Short spur shoots are common. Terminal bud 3 - 6 mm long, egg-shaped with a blunt to rounded tip, scales yellow-brown to red-brown and occasionally hairy. Lateral buds are smaller and point away from the stem. Leaves alternate, usually clustered near ends of branches, 5 - 15 cm long, 2.5 - 10 cm wide, oval to widest above the middle, margins entire to wavy, rarely with few irregular teeth, very shiny and dark green above, pale and usually hairy below. The fall color is spectacular orange, red and purple. Male and female flowers growing on separate trees (dioecious), with the possible occurrence of bisexual flowers. Flowers green-white, on long stalks, male flowers in almost spherical, many-flowered clusters, female flowers in small clusters. Fruit is fleshy with a hard center stone (drupe), solitary or in stalked clusters of 2 or 3, 0.8 - 1.2 cm long, blue-black, oblong.

Similar Species: Nyssa sylvatica can be distinguished from other Chicago Region trees by its very shiny leaves with spectacular orange, red and purple fall color, horizontal branching, bark resembling alligator hide, and short spurs on twigs.

Flowering: Early May to mid-July.

Habitat and Ecology: Locally common in acidic boggy or peaty soils, wet woods, and along swamp margins, but is adaptable to upland sites. This species is a very rare tree in Wisconsin.

Notes: The wood of N. sylvatica is very strong, difficult to split, and used in furniture, veneer, tool handles, docks, and shipping crates. Many birds eat the fruit. Bees collect nectar from the flowers.

Etymology: Nyssa comes from the name of a water nymph in Greek mythology. Sylvatica comes from the Latin word meaning "of the forest."

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Special Concern in Wisconsin
