Gentiana puberulenta J. Pringle
downy gentian, prairie gentian

Synonyms: Gentiana puberula auct. non Michx.

Description: Perennial herb, 20 - 60 cm tall. Stems mostly unbranched, with short hairs often in long lines, especially on the stem angles. Leaves opposite, stalkless, firm, narrow, longer than wide (2 - 7 cm long, 0.5 - 2 cm wide), broadest just above the base, tapering above the middle to the tip, and margins rough-hairy mostly near the base. Flowers stalkless, crowded near the top of stem in clusters of 1 - 6 (also in the upper axils of leaves), deep blue with green tints on the outside, and opened at maturity. Sepal tips (calyx lobes) linear (only about 1 mm wide, 0.4 - 2 cm long), broadest near the very base, and the margins not hairy. Petals five, free tips up to 1.4 cm long, spreading, and alternating with shorter, two-toothed, fused membranes (plaits). Anthers all separate, not fused together.

Similar Species: The only other gentian with stalkless, five-petaled, deep blue flowers that could resemble Gentiana puberulenta is its hybrid with G. andrewsii, G. x billingtonii. The hybrid differs by having wider (more than 5 mm) and longer calyx lobes. Albino forms of G. puberulenta do occur, but they are clearly different from the always whitish-flowered species,Gentiana alba, which has non-hairy stems and leaves (especially smooth leaf margins), flowers almost closed or at least the free tips of the petals not spreading, and the anthers connected to each other.

Flowering: August to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Occasional in mostly dry prairies and prairie remnants, gravel hill prairies, sandy areas that experience a wet and dry seasonality, and less commonly in dry upland woods.

Notes: The frequency of this species decreases in areas where grazing by livestock occurs.

Etymology: Gentiana is named after Gentius, king of Illyria, who supposedly discovered a medicinal value for the plant. Puberulenta derives from the Latin word referring to hairiness.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Listed as Threatened in Indiana and Endangered in Michigan.
