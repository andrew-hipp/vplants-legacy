Pinus nigra Arnold
Austrian pine

Synonyms: Pinus nigra Arnold var. austriaca (Hoess) Aschers. & Graebn.

Description: Tree, 15 - 24 m tall, trunk diameter 45 - 90 cm. Crown uniformly pyramidal. Bark pinkish gray to almost black, thick, becoming coarse. Older trunks deeply fissured and flaking. Twigs stiff, smooth, yellowish brown to olive-brown, becoming darker with age. Buds 1.2 - 2 cm long, cylindrical, slightly pointed, reddish brown with a white resin coating. Needles in clusters of two, incurved, 8 - 16 cm long, dark green, sharp-pointed, thin, flexible (won't snap apart when bent). Reproductive structures borne in cones; male and female cones growing on the same tree (monoecious). Pollination between cones occurs in May and June. Pollen cones small, yellow, cylindrical, clustered at the base of new shoots. Young seed cones small, red, cylindrical, at tips of new shoots. Mature seed cones woody, stalkless, upright, 5 - 8 cm long, lustrous yellow-brown, egg-shaped; scales smooth with a deciduous prickle on the thickened apex. Seeds 6 mm long, reddish brown, with wings 1.5 - 2 cm long.

Similar Species: Pinus nigra is best identified by its pinkish gray bark, large resin-coated terminal buds, long, stiff needles in clusters of two (which won’t snap apart when bent), and yellowish brown, egg-shaped seed cones. It’s distinguished from P. resinosa by having needles that won’t snap apart when bent and yellowish brown seed cones with a prickle.
Pinus resinosa

Habitat and Ecology: Pinus nigra normally occurs in mesic woods and upland dunes, but does not occur naturally in the Chicago Region. The population at Illinois Beach State Park in Lake County, Illinois, was planted in the 1860's in an effort to stabilize the dunes.

Notes: Introduced from Europe. Commonly used for landscape and shade purposes, being hardy and easily transplanted when small. Often planted along highways for its tolerance of air pollution, salt spray, and dry soils. There are many geographic varieties. Diplodia needle-cast disease is common.

Etymology: Pinus is the Latin word for pine. Nigra means black.

Regional Status: Non-native.
