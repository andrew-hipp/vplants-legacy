Inocybe rimosa (Bull: Fr.) Kummer
Rimose Fiber Head, Cracked Inocybe, Striated Inocybe, Straw-colored Fiber Head

Synonyms: Inocybe fastigiata (Schaeff.) Quél.

Description: Gilled mushroom, cap 3 - 6 cm broad; stem 4 - 8 cm long, 5 - 7 mm thick. Cap conical when young, expanding to become flat or even upturned; with a central bump (umbo). Cap surface covered with radiating, medium brown, matted hairs; the hairs form a dark brown center and become widely split (rimose) to the margin, revealing the whitish or light tan under surface. Gills narrow, crowded. Gill margin frosted white (use hand lens). Stem straight, often curving near the rounded base; base lacks a bulb. Odor is spermatic or unpleasant. Spore print dull yellow-brown. Spores smooth, oval, 9 - 13 x 5 - 8 µm. Gill cystidia are thin-walled and clavate.

Similar Species: Inocybe rimosa is distinctive from similar rimose Inocybes primarily by cap color and degree of splitting. Other species have fibers that split more finely into single fibers which remain relatively close. Inocybe rimosa has fibers that split more often into widely-spaced clumps. The dark, intact umbo is also distinctive. Inocybe sororia is quite similar but has an odor of green corn. Spore and cystidia characters are also important.
Inocybe cookei, Inocybe praetervisa, Inocybe sororia

Fruiting: July to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Common in hardwood forest, oak woodland, and prairie with woody plants. Single or in groups on the ground. Ectomycorrhizal partner with oak and other hosts.

Notes: This species is to be expected in most woodlands around Chicago. Many Inocybes are difficult to identify to species and I. rimosa is especially variable across different parts of the world. The above description is the basis for our application of the name Inocybe rimosa to specimens in the Chicago region. In some books this mushroom is found under the name Inocybe fastigiata which is a synonym.

Etymology: Inocybe is derived from the Greek ino, meaning "fiber" and kybe, meaning "head." Rimosa derives from the Latin for "full of cracks or fissures."

References:
- Kaufmann, C. H. 1918. The Gilled Mushrooms (Agaricaceae) of Michigan and the Great Lakes Region. (Dover Publications reprint 1971).
- Phillips, R. 1991. Mushrooms of North America. Little, Brown and Company.
