Lactarius indigo (Schw.) Fr. var. indigo
indigo milky, indigo milk cap, blue milk cap, blue Lactarius

Synonyms: Agaricus indigo Schweinitz; Lactarius canadensis Winder [non Lactarius canadensis A.H. Smith]

Description: Gilled mushroom, Cap 5 - 12 cm broad; stem 3 - 8 cm long, 1 - 2.5 mm thick. Cap broadly convex with depressed center, color varies from indigo blue to pale silvery gray. Cap margin incurved sometimes becoming wavy. Cap surface smooth, sticky. Cap flesh brittle, indigo blue, discoloring greenish when bruised or cut. Gills attached or slightly running down stem apex, close, 2-5 mm broad, pale blue to grayish blue, sometimes appearing yellowish from spores, staining green. Stem equal or tapered to base, straight or curved, surface somewhat sticky becoming dry, often spotted. Stem flesh firm, whitish, immediately turning indigo blue when cut, staining greenish, hollow with age. Latex found throughout flesh of cap, gills, and stem, deep blue to indigo blue, greenish after exposure. Taste mild to slightly acrid. Spore print pale yellow. Spores broadly elliptical to nearly round, ornamentation in the form of a partial reticulum with obtuse warts; prominences 0.5 µm or less high; 7 - 9 x 5.5 - 7.5 µm.

Similar Species: Lactarius mushrooms have brittle flesh and attached gills; they exude a white or colored latex when cut. This species is easily recognized by its blue coloration and indigo blue latex throughout the fruitbody. Lactarius chelidonium having dull orange latex (pine associate) and L. paradoxus having purplish red latex (pine and oak associate) sometimes show bluish caps because both species have a variable amount of blue latex in the cap and sometimes the stem. Neither show the entirely blue latex of L. indigo. Lactarius indigo var. diminutivus is a small variety found in Texas.

Fruiting: July to August.

Habitat and Ecology: Rare, localized, found in hardwood forest or mixed hardwood and pine. Single or in groups on the ground. Ectomycorrhizal partner with oak and pine.

Notes: An example of nomenclature: Lewis D. von Schweinitz, living in North Carolina, named this species in 1822 as Agaricus indigo (back when most gilled mushrooms were put in one genus). Swedish mycologist Elias Fries moved the species to Lactarius in 1838. In 1871 Winder named the same species in Canada as Lactarius canadensis, the "Canadian Blue Fungus." This later name becomes a synonym.

Etymology: lact - arius means milk - possessing. The epithet indigo describes the bright blue milk.
