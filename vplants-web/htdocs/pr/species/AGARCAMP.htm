Agaricus campestris Fries
meadow mushroom, pink bottom, field mushroom

Synonyms: Psalliota campestris (Fr.) Quélet ; Agaricus campester Fr.

Description: Gilled mushroom, cap 3 - 11 cm broad; stem 2 - 6 cm long, 10 - 25 mm thick. Cap convex to broadly convex, sometimes becoming flat with age, white to pale gray. Cap surface smooth, dry, sometimes covered with fine brownish-gray fibers or scales in dry conditions. Cap flesh thick, white, unchanging when bruised or cut, may discolor reddish-brown with age, not staining yellow with KOH. Gills free, crowded to close, narrow, pink when young eventually becoming blackish-brown. Partial veil may leave remnants on cap margin and forms a thin, membranous, quickly collapsing ring on the middle to upper part of stem that typically disappears with age. Stem stout, tapering to a pointed base, white, turning reddish-brown, smooth above ring, covered with fine fibers or hairs below, stuffed to hollow. Odor mild. Taste slightly of anise. Spore print blackish-brown. Spores elliptical, smooth; 6 - 8.5 x 4 - 5.5 µm.

Similar Species: The most common, but not the only Agaricus species in lawns. Agaricus bitorquis has thicker flesh, a double ring, and doesn't have the bright pink gills when young. Other species have scaly or differently colored caps, or yellow staining, or stronger odors. Agaricus bitorquis

Fruiting: July to November.

Habitat and Ecology: Common in lawns and grassy areas. Single to scattered on the ground. Soil saprobe.

Notes: Was also called "common mushroom, champignon, or hot-bed mushroom" when it was thought to be the same species as the common store mushroom Agaricus bisporus, which is from the eastern Mediterranean. This and other urban mushrooms can accumulate chemicals, toxins, and heavy metals from the surrounding soil.

Etymology: "Agaricon" is an ancient Greek word for fungus, reportedly derived from Agara, the name of a Greek city which had abundant mushrooms nearby. "Campestris" is derived from the same word as campus, meaning a field.
