Plant of Concern
Pinus banksiana Lamb.
jack pine, eastern jack pine

Synonyms: Pinus divaricata (Ait.) Dum.-Cours.

Description: Tree, 12 - 22 m tall, trunk diameter 20 - 30 cm. Crown open, irregular. Bark deep reddish brown to dark gray, thin, rough with narrow, scaly ridges. Twigs thin, rough, yellowish green, becoming deep reddish brown. Buds pale brown. Terminal bud 6 mm long, egg-shaped with a rounded apex. Lateral buds smaller. Needles in clusters of two, 2 - 4 cm long, yellowish green, slightly twisted, sharp-pointed, stiff, growing from a short, persistent sheath. Reproductive structures borne in cones; male and female cones growing on the same tree (monoecious). Pollination between cones occurs from early May to early June. Pollen cones small, yellow, cylindrical, clustered at the base of new shoots throughout crown. Young seed cones small, deep purple, spherical, at tips of last year’s shoots in upper and mid-crown. Mature seed cones woody, stalkless, upright, 4 - 7 cm long, brownish yellow, oblong-conical, curved; scales without prickles and thickest at the apex. The cones remain closed for many years or until fire opens them (serotinous). Seeds 3 mm long, blackish, triangular, with wings about 10 mm long.

Similar Species: Pinus banksiana is best identified by its short, stiff, yellowish green needles in clusters of two, and its curved, closed seed cones which persist on the tree with open cones. P. pungens has slightly longer (5 - 10 cm), bluish green needles and heavy, egg-shaped seed cones with thick, prickled scales. P. sylvestris has distinctly orange-brown bark in the mid and upper trunk, bluish green needles, and egg-shaped seed cones with scales that thicken at the apex into a 4-sided, unarmed point. P. virginiana has soft, flexible, grayish green needles and reddish brown, egg-shaped seed cones armed with a long prickle.
Pinus pungens, Pinus sylvestris, Pinus virginiana

Habitat and Ecology: In the Chicago Region, Pinus banksiana seldom occurs far from Lake Michigan and usually occurs in areas with a history of fire. It occurs in foredunes and higher, more mature dune areas in dry woodlands, but is most common around the edges of interdunal ponds behind the first major dunes (pannes).

Notes: Pinus banksiana is a fire-adapted species; heat from fire enables the serotinous, resin-bonded cones to open, and the seeds germinate on the denuded ground. The wood is used for fence posts, railroad ties, pulpwood, fuel, and boxes.

Etymology: Pinus is the Latin word for pine. Banksiana refers to and honors Sir Joseph Banks (1743-1820), a distinguished botanist.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Endangered in Illinois, Rare in Indiana.
