Invasive Plant
Ailanthus altissima (P. Mill.) Swingle
tree of heaven

Synonyms: Ailanthus glandulosa Desf.

Description: Tree, up to 25 m tall. Bark gray with thin, light vertical streaks. Twigs stout, yellow- to red-brown, covered with velvety hairs. Leaf scar very large and heart-shaped. Terminal bud lacking; lateral buds very small, red-brown, hairy. Leaves pinnately compound, up to 1 m long, with 11 - 41 leaflets. Leaflets red to purplish when emerging, becoming dark green, narrow egg-shaped with squared base, margins entire except for 1 - 4 teeth near the base, usually with a gland on the underside of each tooth. Twigs and leaves release a strong, unpleasant odor when crushed. Male and female flowers usually on separate trees (dioecious), borne at the end of a shoot on an inflorescence, 10 - 30 cm long. Florets 5 mm wide, yellow-green, male flowers unpleasantly scented, female flowers odorless. Fruit winged (samara), 3 - 5 cm long, 7 - 13 mm wide, yellow-green to orange-red, twisted, with a single seed in the center. Large clusters of samaras persist into winter.

Similar Species: Ailanthus altissima may be mistaken for the native species Rhus typhina as both have large pinnately compound leaves. Ailanthus altissima has winged fruits hanging in large clusters, very large heart-shaped leaf scars, leaflets with a few small glandular teeth near the base and no fall color. Rhus typhina has upright, pyramidal clusters of spherical, red, hairy fruits; leaflets with regularly toothed margins; and yellow, orange and red fall color. Rhus typhina

Flowering: Mid-June to mid-July.

Habitat and Ecology: Adaptable to very poor soils and pollution, this species is often found in industrial and commerical areas, older residential areas, alleys, between buildings, along walls, around dumps, along streams, and in disturbed natural areas.

Notes: Ailanthus altissima was introduced as a landscape tree in the 1780s and has become invasive. It is capable of growing in the poorest of soils, and its root system is strong enough to damage foundations and sewers. A single plant of this invasive species produces up to 325,000 seeds per year. It also spreads by root sprouts, and is capable of growing over 1.5 m per year. To prevent invasion of its territory, A. altissima emits toxins that kill other plant species.

Etymology: Ailanthus comes from the Moluccan name for this tree. Altissima comes from the Latin words meaning very tall.

Regional Status: Non-native. Invasive.
