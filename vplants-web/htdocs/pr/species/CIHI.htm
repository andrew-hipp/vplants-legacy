Plant of Concern
Cirsium hillii (Canby) Fernald
Hill's thistle, prairie thistle, hollow-rooted thistle

Synonyms: Cnicus hillii Canby; Cirsium pumilum (Nutt.) Spreng. subsp. hillii (Canby) R.J. Moore & Frankton; Cirsium pumilum var. hillii (Canby) B. Boivin

Description: Perennial herb with stout roots, 25 - 60 cm tall. Roots elongated, thickened, and hollow. Stem relatively delicate and unbranched. Leaves of two kinds: a rosette of basal leaves and alternately arranged stem leaves. Basal leaves persistent, large, 12 - 25 cm long, 2 - 7 cm wide, with reddish midveins. Stem leaves a bit smaller, green beneath, wavy-lobed with numerous short marginal spines and scattered larger ones intermixed. Flowering heads few, large, with the involucre about 3.5 - 7 cm wide and more than 3.5 cm tall. Spines on outer series of phyllaries 1.5 - 3.5 mm long and erect. Individual flowers in each head rich rose-purple.

Similar Species: Cirsium hillii can be distinguished from other pinkish flowered thistles by its large, yet relatively few flowering heads, stems lacking spiny wings, and the leaves being green beneath. Both C. muticum and C. arvense can be confused with C. hillii, but they normally have more than 5 flowering heads and these less than 3 cm broad, less than 3.5 cm high, and the spines on the phyllaries less than 1 mm long. However, when flowers are not present these three species can often be confused.
Cirsium arvense, Cirsium muticum

Flowering: June to August.

Habitat and Ecology: Dry prairies and other open places, often in sandy soil, but rare in the Chicago Region.

Notes: The original collection of C. hillii was from a Chicago Region locale, Pine Station (now part of Gary) in Lake County, Indiana. Reverend Ellsworth J. Hill, a common Chicago area collector near the turn of the 19th-20th centuries, collected this plant in 1890 and it was named for him. This specimen, called a type specimen, is deposited in the herbarium at the New York Botanical Garden and is available for viewing on-line. Many current authorities believe this taxon is not a separate species from the eastern C. pumilum, under which it has been placed as either a subspecies or variety, still retaining the epithet hillii. Today, the continued existence of C. hillii is under threat from habitat loss. Fire suppression is also thought to have severely diminished populations of this species since it requires open bare soil for seedlings to develop.
View type specimen of C. hillii at the New York Botanical Garden

Etymology: Cirsium comes from both the Greek words kirsion, meaning a kind of thistle, and cirsos, meaning "swollen vein" for which thistles were once thought a remedy. Hillii is named after Rev. E. J. Hill (1833 - 1917), a respected botanist who first collected this plant in 1890.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Endangered in Indiana, Special Concern in Michigan, Threatened in Wisconsin.
