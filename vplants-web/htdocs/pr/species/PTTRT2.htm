Ptelea trifoliata L. ssp. trifoliata var. trifoliata
common hoptree

Synonyms: Ptelea trifoliata L., sensu Fernald, also Swink & Wilhelm

Description: Shrub, occasionally a small tree, to 7.5 m tall, trunk to 20 cm in diameter. Bark gray to brown and slightly rough, developing cracks with age. Twigs yellow-green to red-brown, warty, with very few to no hairs. Leaf scars are large and horseshoe-shaped. Buds tiny, spherical, light brown, wooly. Leaves alternate, stalked, with 3 leaflets (trifoliate). Leaflets 10 - 15 cm long, 5 - 10 cm wide, egg-shaped with a long, pointed tip, margins entire or finely toothed, dark green above, paler with very few to no hairs below. Male, female and perfect flowers all on the same plant, usually all in the same stalked cluster, each with 3 - 5 green-white petals. Fruit is a winged seed (samara), 2 - 2.5 cm wide, yellow-green changing to brown, flattened and rounded, resembling a wafer, borne in hanging clusters that persist into winter. Leaves, twigs and fruit smell unpleasant.

Similar Species: Ptelea trifoliata ssp. trifoliata var. trifoliata differs from P. trifoliata ssp. trifoliata var. mollis because it has very few to no hairs on its twigs and leaves. The leaves of this shrub resemble those of the vine Toxicodendron radicans (poison ivy).

Flowering: Mid-May to late June.

Habitat and Ecology: Open areas, dry woods, edges of woods, rocky bluffs with limestone near the surface, and along roads and fencerows.

Notes: Beer brewers once used P. trifoliata fruit as a hops substitute.

Etymology: Ptelea is the Greek name for an elm tree, referring to the similarity of the winged fruits to those of an elm. Trifoliata comes from the Latin words tres, meaning three, and folium, meaning leaf, referring to the trifoliate leaves.

Regional Status: Native.
