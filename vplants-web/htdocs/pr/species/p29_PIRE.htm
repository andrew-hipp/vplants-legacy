Pinus resinosa Soland.

Common Names: red pine

Description: Tree, 20 - 45 m tall, trunk diameter to 0.9 m. Crown pyramidal. Bark reddish to pinkish brown, thick, flaky, divided irregularly into scaly plates. Twigs orange-brown, becoming rough with scales. Buds reddish brown, about 2 cm long, egg-shaped or conical, slightly pointed, with loose scales. Needles in clusters of two, lustrous dark green, 10 - 17 cm long, sharp-pointed, straight, flexible (but will snap apart when bent), growing from an elongated, persistent sheath. Pollen cones purple, 1.3 - 2 cm long, cylindrical, clustered at the base of last year's shoots in mid-crown. Young seed cones red, small, spherical, near tips of new shoots in upper crown. Pollination between cones occurs in April and May. Mature seed cones woody, brown, about 5 cm long, egg-shaped, nearly stalkless. Scales thickest at the apex and lack prickles. Seeds mottled brown, about 3 mm long, oval to triangular, with wings 1 - 1.5 cm long. Go to Plant Glossary

Similar Species: Pinus resinosa is best identified by its flaky, reddish to pinkish brown bark, its long, flexible, lustrous dark green needles in clusters of two (which snap apart when bent), and its small, egg-shaped, unarmed seed cones with mottled brown seeds. It's distinguished from P. nigra by having needles that snap apart when bent and brown (not yellowish brown) seed cones without prickles. Pinus nigra

Habitat and Ecology: In the Chicago Region, this species was only known from Lake Forest in Lake County, Illinois, where it occurred near the base of a clay bluff. It occurs along sandstone bluffs in areas west of the Chicago Region.

Notes: Pinus resinosa is a common ornamental and shade tree, easily cultivated in nurseries. The disease Gremmeniella canker, caused by Gremmeniella abietina, affects young trees. The wood is used for general construction, bridges, ships, ladders, pulpwood, poles, and interior trim.

Etymology: Pinus is the Latin word for pine. Resinosa means resinous.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Endangered in Illinois.

vPlants Taxon Code: PIRE
