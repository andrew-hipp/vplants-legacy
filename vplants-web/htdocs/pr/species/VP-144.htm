Aster ericoides L. ssp. ericoides var. ericoides
white heath aster

Synonyms: Aster ericoides L. var. ericoides ; Aster multiflorus Ait. ; Symphyotrichum ericoides (L.) Nesom var. ericoides

Description: Perennial herb, 0.4 - 1 m tall. Stems one to few, erect, thin, covered with few soft hairs, branches somewhat arching. Basal rosette leaves unstalked, 2 - 6 cm long, greenish gray, spatula-shaped, margins entire, surface sparsely and softly hairy. The basal leaves and lower stem leaves die back at flowering time. Stem leaves unstalked, 3 - 10 cm long, linear, margins entire, surface sparsely and softly hairy, with clusters of small leaves often in axils of larger ones. Inflorescence pyramidal, especially at the top. Peduncles 1 - 5 cm long, densely hairy. Involucre 3 - 5 mm tall, cylindrical to narrowly cup-shaped. Phyllaries in 3 - 4 distinct rows; outer row 1 - 1.5 mm long, upper portion green, spatula-shaped with sparse, soft hairs. Ray florets (8 - 20) white, rarely pink, 4 - 7 mm long. Disc florets yellow (turning brown to purplish after flowering), 2.5 - 4 mm long, narrowly funnel-shaped. Pappus single-whorled, about as long as the disc corolla. Achenes 1.2 - 2 mm long, 0.4 - 0.6 mm wide, 7 - 9 ribbed, purple turning brown with weathering.

Similar Species: Aster ericoides spp. ericoides var. ericoides has sparse, soft hairs on the stem, leaves, and pyllaries, while A. ericoides spp. ericoides var. prostratus has dense, stiff hairs. Another similar taxon is A. pilosum var. pilosum which has larger heads, longer ray florets, and phyllaries that are not spine-tipped.
Aster ericoides, Aster ericoides ssp. ericoides var. prostratus, A. pilosum var. pilosum

Flowering: Mid-August to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Common in dry prairie remnants, edges of roads, and railways.

Notes: A number of aster cultivars are sold with the name "Aster ericoides." However, these are derived from European asters and are not native to North America. The misapplication occurred in the 19th century and has persisted in the horticultural industry ever since.

Etymology: Aster comes from the Greek word for star, referring to the appearance of the flower head. Ericoides means resembling Erica or heath.

Regional Status: Native.
