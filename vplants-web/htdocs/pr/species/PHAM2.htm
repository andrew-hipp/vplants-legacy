Phellodendron amurense Rupr.
Amur cork tree, Amur corktree

Description: Tree, 9 - 14 m tall, as wide or wider than tall. Bark gray-brown, becoming furrowed and corky with age. Twigs orange-yellow to yellow-gray, becoming brown, covered with prominent corky spots (lenticels), strongly scented when scratched. Leaf scars U-shaped, nearly surrounding the buds. Buds 3 mm long, elliptic, scales covered with reddish hairs. Leaves opposite, pinnately compound, 25 - 38 cm long, with 5 - 11 leaflets. Leaflets 6.3 - 11.5 cm long, 3 - 6 cm wide, egg-shaped to narrow egg-shaped, margins entire, shiny dark green, turning yellow-bronze in fall. Male and female flowers on separate plants (dioecious), borne in loose, branched inflorescences (panicles), yellow-green. Fruit is fleshy with a hard stone in the center (drupe), borne in loose clusters, 1 cm in diameter, nearly spherical, black, each stone containing 5 seeds. Fruit is aromatic when bruised and persists into winter.

Similar Species: Phellodendron amurense is rarely seen outside of cultivation, but is easy to distinguish from other species in the Chicago Region. It has corky bark, strongly scented twigs and leaves, and buds almost completely surrounded by the leaf scars. Female trees also have strongly scented, black fleshy fruit containing a 5-seeded stone.

Flowering: May.

Habitat and Ecology: Escaped cultivation at the Morton Arboretum, with wild plants found on the Arboretum grounds and forest preserves nearby.

Notes: The cork used to stop wine bottles and make bulletin boards is not produced by this species, but rather from Quercus suber, the cork-oak, native to the Mediterranean.

Etymology: Phellodendron comes from the Greek words phellos, meaning cork, and dendron, meaning tree, referring to the corky bark. Amurense means from the region in eastern Asia near the Amur River.

Regional Status: Non-native.
