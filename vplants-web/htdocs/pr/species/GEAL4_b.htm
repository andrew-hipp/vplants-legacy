Gentiana alba Muhl. ex Nutt.
plain gentian, yellowish gentian

Synonyms: Gentiana flavida Gray

Key Features: stem non-hairy; leaves opposite, stalkless, and hairless; flowers stalkless, almost closed, with five whitish petals; sepal tips with smooth edges and a ridge down the outside; anthers united.

Description: Perennial herb, 30 cm - 1 m tall. Stem stout, normally unbranched, hairless. Leaves opposite, spreading, stalkless, 2 - 5 cm wide, 5 - 15 cm long, yellow-green, egg-shaped with long pointy tips, wide bases, and smooth margins. Flowers (1 - 20) compactly clustered at the top of the stem, 3.5 - 5 cm tall, yellow-white or green-white, and almost closed. Free tips of sepals (calyx lobes) 3.5 - 15 mm long, 2 - 7 mm wide, with a ridge-like structure (keel) running down the outer face to the connected tube portion. Petals five, but fused with shorter membranes (plaits) between the actual petals. Anthers fused together.

Similar Species: This is the only whitish-flowered gentian in our area except for the rare albino of other species. Albino forms of Gentiana andrewsii or its varieties have rough-prickly leaf margins, hairy margined calyx lobes, and plaits longer than the alternating petals. Albino forms of G. puberulenta may also be similar, but they have a hairy stem, very narrow calyx lobes, opened flowers, and separate anthers.
Gentiana andrewsii, Gentiana andrewsii var andrewsii, Gentiana andrewsii var. dakotica, Gentiana puberulenta

Flowering: August to October.

Habitat and Ecology: Moist prairies, sometimes swales or other wet or moist clearings, but uncommon overall.

Etymology: Gentiana is named after Gentius, king of Illyria, who supposedly discovered a medicinal value for the plant. Alba is the Latin word for white, which refers to the whitish flowers.

Regional Status: Native.

Regional Conservation Status: Listed as Rare in Indiana, Endangered in Michigan, and Threatened in Wisconsin.
