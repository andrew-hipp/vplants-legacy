Agaricus bitorquis Pilát
sidewalk mushroom, Rodman's mushroom, spring agaric, urban agaric

Synonyms: Agaricus edulis; Agaricus rodmanii Peck; Psalliota rodmani (Peck) Kauffman

Description: Gilled mushroom, cap 4 - 16 cm broad; stem 4 - 10 cm long, 10 - 35 mm thick. Cap convex to flat, sometimes with center depressed, white or whitish, discoloring yellowish to tan with age. Cap margin inrolled when young, eventually becoming upturned. Cap surface dry, smooth, sometimes cracking to form scales. Cap flesh thick, firm, white, not staining yellow with KOH. Gills free or nearly free, close, very narrow, whitish to grayish pink becoming deep reddish-brown, chocolate brown, and eventually blackish-brown. Partial veil membranous, thick, white, persistent at middle or base of stem, appearing as a double ring or sheathing with the top edge flaring. Stem equal or slightly tapered to apex, base is typically pointed, white, smooth, sometimes with fine appressed scales toward the top. Stem flesh solid, firm. Odor and taste mild. Spore print chocolate brown. Spores elliptical, smooth; 5 - 6.5 x 4 - 5.5 µm. Cystidia present and numerous.

Similar Species: The tightly inrolled cap margin typically leaves a second ring on the stem below the ring formed by the partial veil. This feature plus the usually large size and thick stems will separate it from similar Agaricus species that have smooth, white caps, and lack strong staining. Agaricus campestris

Fruiting: May to November.

Habitat and Ecology: Typically found in urban areas or city parks. Single, several, or in large groups on the ground in lawns, grassy areas, and often next to sidewalks and trails. Soil saprobe.

Notes: Agaricus bitorquis seems to prefer hard-packed or disturbed soil. The buttons start forming below ground so the mushrooms are typically covered with dirt when they emerge. It can fruit several times a year in the same location with ample rainfall. This and other urban mushrooms can accumulate chemicals, toxins, and heavy metals from the surrounding soil.

Etymology: "Agaricon" is an ancient Greek word for fungus, reportedly derived from Agara, the name of a Greek city which had abundant mushrooms nearby. "Bitorquis" refers to the double ring.
